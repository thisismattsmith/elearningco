# Fight or flight. 

## Panic or action. 

Follow procedure or improvise.

Those 30 seconds are determined by training.

### If people are trained:

- They know their role immediately
- They act on procedure, not panic
- They make sound decisions under stress
- They coordinate with others effectively
- They protect themselves and others

### If people aren't trained:

They freeze, uncertain what to do
They make dangerous assumptions
They wait for someone else to act
They follow the crowd (which might be wrong)
Panic spreads

**The difference between orderly evacuation and chaos? Between controlled response and disaster? Between minor incident and tragedy?**

It usually comes down to whether people knew what to do in those first 30 seconds.
You can't train someone during an emergency. You have to train them before.
And one verbal briefing at induction isn't training - it's information they'll forget under stress.

Real training creates muscle memory. When the alarm sounds, hands move to the right actions without thinking. When medical emergency happens, first aiders respond automatically. 

When shelter's required, people move to designated areas.
That only happens through:

- Realistic scenarios that simulate pressure
- Practice of procedures until automatic
- Visual memory of site and equipment
- Clear roles so everyone knows their part

That's what emergency response training should do. And that's what most organisations don't have.

## Emergency Response Training That Prepares Your Team for Crisis

Most emergency response training goes like this:

"Here's the evacuation map. Here's the assembly point. Pull the alarm if there's fire. Call 000 for medical emergencies. Any questions?"

Then an actual emergency happens and people:

- Freeze because they're uncertain what to do first
- Can't remember procedures because they only heard them once
- Make dangerous decisions under stress
- Don't know role-specific responsibilities
- Waste critical time figuring out who's in charge
- Panic spreads because nobody seems prepared

Telling people what to do isn't the same as preparing them to act.

### Our Approach: Emergency Response That Becomes Instinct

We build emergency response training that creates confident crisis response:

#### Scenario-Based Training

Not abstract "if there's a fire" but realistic scenarios that walk through decision-making, actions, and coordination in specific emergencies.

#### Your Site, Your Procedures

Not generic evacuation but your specific building layout, assembly points, emergency equipment locations, site-specific hazards.

#### Role-Specific Responsibilities

What fire wardens do. What first aiders do. What managers do. What everyone else does. Clear accountability.

#### Practice Under Pressure

Interactive scenarios that simulate stress, time pressure, and incomplete information - closer to real emergency conditions.
#### Common Mistakes Prevented

What people typically get wrong in emergencies. Dangerous assumptions. Panic responses. We address them proactively.
